About This Program

You just heard about this at convention, or someone at the shop mentioned "apprenticeships and funding." Here's what you need to know.

What This Apprenticeship Program Is

A nationally registered apprenticeship program for avionics technicians, approved by the U.S. Department of Labor under 29 CFR Part 29. This is the real deal — not a certificate program, not an industry credential — a DOL-registered apprenticeship recognized in all 50 states.

The partnership: AEA (Aircraft Electronics Association) partnered with Fastport — a DOL-recognized Standards Recognition Entity — to create a national program so individual shops don't have to spend 12+ months getting their own apprenticeship approved. You sign one page. Fastport handles DOL compliance. AEA provides the training. You train your people the way you already do.

Why an AEA Shop Owner Should Care

  • Unlock federal and state funding you can't access any other way. Tax credits, wage reimbursements, veteran benefits — registered apprenticeship is the key that opens those doors. Use the Funding Finder to see what's available in your state.
  • Attract veterans. GI Bill pays them $1,500–$3,000+/month on top of wages. SkillBridge gives you 180 days of free labor. Your job offer becomes 40% more valuable than the shop down the street.
  • Structure you probably already need. If you're training people informally anyway, this formalizes it with a clear checklist, milestones, and documentation — making training repeatable and scalable.
  • DOL credential for your apprentice. When they finish, they get a nationally recognized Certificate of Completion. That's retention insurance — they're more likely to stay when they've invested 4 years and earned a credential.
  • Pipeline for A&P certification. The program aligns with FAA A&P requirements. Your apprentice gains practical experience, structured training, and a clear path to FAA certification and Inspection Authorization.

How to Get Started (Simple 3-Step Process)

  1. Sign the Employer Acceptance Agreement — One page. Fastport sends it to you. Sign it. You're now a participating employer under the national program. No separate DOL application needed.
  2. Hire or enroll an apprentice — Could be someone you're hiring new, or a current employee you want to formalize training for. Veterans qualify for Direct Entry (they skip the line). Fastport provides the apprentice agreement form (ETA-671).
  3. Train them and track progress — You handle OJT (the bench work — 43 competencies your shop already teaches). AEA handles RTI (the classroom/online training — 586 hours through CAET). The tracking system makes documentation simple.

💡 Bottom line: This is what every shop owner who's been in business 10+ years wishes they'd set up on Day 1. Structured training, federal money, veteran pipeline, DOL-backed credentials. It's the framework you need to scale your operation without losing quality.

How the Program Works

A competency-based registered apprenticeship with two components: On-the-Job Training (OJT) and Related Technical Instruction (RTI). Here's what each one means and who handles it.

🔧

OJT — On-the-Job Training

Who handles it: Your shop.

43 competencies across 8 skill domains. The apprentice works alongside a journeyworker learning by doing — the same way you already train people. The apprenticeship just formalizes it with a checklist and sign-offs.

📚

RTI — Related Technical Instruction

Who handles it: AEA.

586 hours of structured classroom/online training across 4 phases. This is the theory, the regulations, the systems knowledge. AEA provides this through CAET training — your shop doesn't have to build a curriculum.

📋

Fastport — Compliance

Who handles it: Fastport.

All DOL registration, EEO compliance, reporting, and administrative paperwork. Your shop signs one page — the Employer Acceptance Agreement — and Fastport handles the rest.

🎯

Competency-Based

What it means: Advancement is based on demonstrated skill, not just time served.

Each competency is rated 0-5 by a qualified evaluator. The apprentice advances when they can DO the work, not when a calendar says so. Faster learners can finish faster.

🎖️ Veterans & Military

Direct entry. Veterans and Job Corps graduates can bypass standard selection procedures. Military avionics training counts — credit for previous experience reduces your program term. GI Bill pays the apprentice $1,500–$3,000+/month on top of wages. COOL pays up to $4,000/year for certifications. SkillBridge provides up to 180 days of free labor during transition.

Wage Progression

Competency-based wage increases. The apprentice advances when they demonstrate skill, not when a calendar says so.

Phase Competencies Hourly Wage % of Journeyworker Annual (est.)
Phase 1 1 – 10 $23.00 77% $47,840
Phase 2 11 – 20 $24.00 80% $49,920
Phase 3 21 – 30 $25.00 83% $52,000
Phase 4 31 – 43 $26.00 87% $54,080
Journeyworker Complete $30.00 100% $62,400

💡 These Are Minimums

The wage schedule above is the DOL-registered minimum. Your shop can — and should — pay more based on your local market. Many shops will start higher. The key is that wages must progress as competencies are demonstrated.

🎖️ Veterans Stack Benefits

A veteran apprentice earning $23/hr also receives GI Bill housing allowance ($1,500–$3,000+/month depending on ZIP). That's effectively $32–$41/hr total compensation in Phase 1 — at no cost to the shop.

📋 Employer Flexibility

Shops can modify the wage scale when they sign the Employer Acceptance Agreement. If your journeyworker rate is $40/hr, adjust the progression accordingly. The percentages stay the same.

Competency Rating System

Each OJT competency is rated on a 0–5 scale by a qualified evaluator. Here's what each rating means.

5
Exceeds All Expectations
Consistently exceeds performance standards. Achieves results above and beyond what is required. Goes beyond their role to deliver exceptional results.
4
Meets & Exceeds Some Expectations
Meets all expectations in a fully satisfactory way and exceeds some objectives. Solid performer with areas of excellence.
3
Meets Expectations
Consistently meets performance standards. Handles routine tasks and some unexpected situations with usual supervision. Can continue to develop with coaching and experience.
2
Meets Some Expectations
Occasionally meets some objectives but not all in a satisfactory way. Generally indicates need for additional coaching, training, or performance improvement plan.
1
Does Not Meet / Meets Some Expectations
Does not consistently meet performance standards. Requires basic training, coaching, or experience to improve. Additional follow-up necessary.
0
Does Not Meet Expectations
Clearly and repeatedly fails to meet performance standards. Specific suggestions for improvement mandatory. Additional follow-up required.

Who Evaluates?

A qualified journeyworker who has worked directly with the apprentice on the competency being evaluated. The evaluator signs off with date, rating, and initials.

When to Evaluate?

Evaluate when the apprentice has had sufficient practice and training on a competency. Don't rush it. A rating of 3 (Meets Expectations) is the minimum for sign-off. Below 3 means more training is needed.

Probationary Period

12 weeks. During probation, either party can cancel the apprenticeship with written notice. After probation, cancellation requires good cause, due notice, and opportunity for corrective action.

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about funding, paperwork, and program mechanics. For the full FAQ, visit faq.html.

How does my shop receive funding payments?

Each funding stream has its own process. Registered apprenticeship is a "ticket to the dance" — it qualifies you for funding, but each source has different triggers and timelines.

  • State tax credits: You claim them on your annual state tax return. The credit reduces what you owe (or in some states like Colorado, you get a refund even if you owe zero).
  • WIOA/workforce board funds: Reimbursement payments after you submit proof of wages paid and training completed. Your local American Job Center administers this.
  • VA benefits (GI Bill): Paid directly to the veteran apprentice by the VA. $0 hits your books — it's supplemental income for them.
  • Federal tax credits (WOTC): Claimed on your federal income tax return after filing IRS Form 8850 within 28 days of hire.

What triggers the actual funding?

It varies by source:

  • State tax credits: Trigger automatically once you have a registered apprentice. You claim it when you file taxes. Fastport provides the documentation you need.
  • WIOA funds: Require coordination with your local American Job Center. You sign an OJT agreement with them before hiring. They reimburse based on milestones (usually monthly or quarterly).
  • VA benefits: Flow directly to the veteran once your shop is VA-approved and the veteran applies. Fastport provides technical assistance navigating VA approval.

Fastport provides technical assistance and coaching throughout all of this. You're not navigating it alone.

What does Fastport do to help with VA approval?

Fastport's subject matter experts provide technical assistance around becoming VA-approved, connecting you with State Approving Agencies (SAA), and navigating the approval process. This is critical if you want to hire veterans who will use GI Bill benefits.

The SAA reviews your training program, ensures it meets VA standards, and issues approval. Once approved, any veteran you hire can apply for GI Bill housing allowance ($1,500–$3,000+/month) while working for you.

Do I need to work with my local workforce board?

Yes, for local WIOA/workforce development funds. Your local American Job Center (part of the workforce board system) administers WIOA On-the-Job Training (OJT) reimbursement — which can reimburse up to 50% of wages during training.

Fastport provides assistance and coaching to help you connect with and navigate your local workforce board requirements. Each board operates slightly differently, so local guidance matters.

What internal commitments are required for state programs?

This varies by state. The National Program Standard provides some reciprocity, but each state and local workforce board may have their own requirements:

  • Some states require annual reporting (Fastport handles this)
  • Some workforce boards require pre-approval before hiring (sign OJT agreement first)
  • Some states have specific documentation requirements for tax credit claims (Fastport provides templates)

Fastport provides guides during onboarding training and continuous technical assistance specific to your state and local requirements.

What paperwork is needed when I approve a new apprentice?

You'll need the ETA-671 (Apprentice Agreement) and Voluntary Disability Disclosure Forms. Successful employers incorporate these into their existing onboarding process — same packet as W-4, I-9, and direct deposit forms.

The forms are individualized (pre-filled with your shop info and apprentice info) and available after Fastport's onboarding training, which covers exactly how to complete and submit them.

Who is responsible for submitting apprentice paperwork?

You (the employer):

  • Hire the apprentice
  • Verify previous training/experience (for credit toward competencies)
  • Support RTI completion (ensure apprentice attends training)
  • Provide qualified mentors and OJT supervision
  • Assess competencies and provide ratings
  • Advise Fastport of status changes: cancelled, suspended, or completed

Fastport:

  • Handles all DOL submissions
  • Manages compliance reporting
  • EEO data collection and reporting
  • DOL registration and deregistration

What records do I need to keep?

Employers retain all apprentice documents including:

  • Hiring records (application, interview notes, selection justification)
  • Signed apprenticeship agreement (ETA-671)
  • Related training documentation (RTI attendance, test scores, completion certificates)
  • OJT records (competency evaluations, supervisor sign-offs, progress tracking)
  • Wage information (pay stubs, wage progression documentation)
  • Competency assessments (ratings, evaluations, milestone completions)

The AEA tracking system maintains most of this digitally. You just need to retain copies per your normal business record retention policy (typically 3–7 years depending on state).

What funding should I pursue as an employer?

Pursue ALL available funding. Don't leave money on the table. Here's the checklist:

  1. WOTC (Work Opportunity Tax Credit) — Federal tax credit for hiring from target groups (veterans, SNAP recipients, long-term unemployed). File IRS Form 8850 within 28 days of hire.
  2. WIOA funds — Contact your local American Job Center and set up an OJT agreement BEFORE you hire. They reimburse up to 50% of wages.
  3. State tax credits — Automatic in most states once you have a registered apprentice. Claim on annual state tax return. Check your state in the Funding Finder.
  4. VA Benefits (if hiring veteran) — Work with Fastport to get VA-approved. Veteran applies for GI Bill. They get $1,500–$3,000+/month.
  5. Veteran-specific tax credits — Some states offer additional credits for hiring veterans. Check the Funding Finder.
  6. Recently announced incentive funding — DOL Pay-for-Performance, ABA grants, state-specific programs. Fastport keeps you updated.

💡 Pro Tip: Use the Funding Finder tool to see exactly what's available in your state. It lists every program with step-by-step application instructions and links to official sources.
